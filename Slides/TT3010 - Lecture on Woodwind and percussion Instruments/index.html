    Resonances in cylindrical OPEN pipes


  • POSITIVE pulse reflected as NEGATIVE pulse
  • OPEN ends*: Atmos. Pres. ($p_s$ = 0 Pa)
  • MIN. displacement = MAX. pressure
  • ALL harmonics $n=1,2,3, \dots$

    • *: End correction ($0.61\cdot r$, r = pipe's radius)

    * Actually, a bit BEYOND THE END, so the ACOUSTIC LENGTH is GREATER than REAL LENGTH. Usually called EFFECTIVE LENGTH.

    Resonances in cylindrical CLOSED pipes


  • POSITIVE pulse reflected as POSITIVE pulse
  • CLOSED end: MAX Pres.
  • FUNDAMENTAL 1 octave LOWER than $f_0$ of OPEN pipe
  • ODD harmonics $n=1,3,5, \dots$

    BRASS instruments: adding the BELL

    Let's now add the bell to the cylinder and evaluate what happens in term of resonances.
    The BRASS instruments are all OPEN tubes, so ALL harmonics from the fundaental could in principle are created.

    BRASS instruments: adding the BELL

  • Peaks towards LOWER frequencies (non-harm.)

  • Amplitude of peaks: higher peaks more lowered than lower peaks

  • Virtual disappearance peaks > 1500 Hz

    • - Impedance: sound PRESSURE / volume VELOCITY (velocity normally constant)
    - PEAKS are SHIFTED toward LOWER freq. by inserting the BELL (DIFFERENT than if we had LENGTHENING the tube!)
    - virtual DISAPPEARANCE of peaks above 1500 HZ (because HIGHER freq. become more PROMINENT on the RADIATED sound --> more Efficient RADIATIOn of HIGH FREQ: by the BELL)
    - HIGHER peaks LOWERED proportionally MORE than LOWER peacks (property of FALRED HORNS) --> EFFECTIVE length INCREASES with FREQUENCY!!!

    BRASS instruments: adding the MOUTHPIECE

    BRASS instruments: adding the MOUTHPIECE

    • Peaks at LOWER resonances are enhanced
    • Lowest resonance of mouthpiece (750-800 Hz)
    • Design bell & mouthpiece: HARMONIC relationship (air column resonances = PARTIALS of tone)
    - HIGH PEAKS great for the control of the lips of the player!
    Notes ABOVE F5 (after 8 + 1) become difficult to play because there are no strong resonances and then they are too weak to help controlling the lips and stabilizing the tone

    BRASS instruments: adding the MOUTHPIECE

    Turning point (used to calculate the EFFECTIVE LENGTH) depends on FREQUENCY:
    "Turning point" - where the EFFECTIVE LENGTH is define is DEPENDING on the FREQUENCY!! This affects the amplitude of peaks and the difficulty to build a good DESIGN!
    Many BRASS players use to insert the hand inside the bell to "TUNE IT" and this changes the amplitudes of the SPECTRUM

    BRASS instruments: Valves & Slides

    To play the remaining notes from the scales (other than the peaks), the ACOUSTICAL LENGTH of the instruments must be varied
    2 ways:
    1- moving a slide (trombone)
    2- inserting lengths of ADDITIONAL TUBING by means of Valves
    - hole in the piston called WINDWAY
    - piston UP: windway xtends the AIR COLUMN Straight though the valve
    - piston DOWN: 2 other windways connect the air column to and ADDITIONAL LENGTH of tubing

    SLIDE: 7 positions to play ALL desired notes of teh SCALE
    - positions not EQUALLY spaced (going down a semitone is DECREASING the FREQ about 6% so the instrument should be lengthen about the same amount )
    But as the instrument gets longer, a 6% of increase in length also becomes PROGRESSIVELY LONGER.

    VALVES: - Challenge to decide the length of extra tubing (p.227 Sience, rossing)

    WOODWIND instruments: Pipe-Reed System

    • Period of lowest mode: $$T_1 =\displaystyle{\frac{4L}{v}},\quad f_1 =\displaystyle{\frac{1}{T_1}}=\displaystyle{\frac{v}{4L}}$$ L = Mouthpiece + pipe's length
      $T_1$ and $f_1$: period and fund.
    • Odd- harmonics: $$f_n = nf_1, \quad n = 1,3,5,\dots$$
    As blowing pressure is applied, the reed valve allows a PUFF OF AIR to flow into the instrument and AT THE SAME TIME starts the REED SWINGING "shut" toward the mouthpiece 12.2 a.
    At d) the NEGATIVE pulse "PULLS" the reed towards the mouthpiece so it is CLOSED, very little air enters and NEGATIVE pulse is reflected.
    When POSITIVE pulse arrives back, the reed is swinging OPEN and the pressure "PUSHES" further open, so that a NEW PUFF of AIR can be introduce from the payer's mouth.
    The air in the pipe has APPRECIABLE MASS, and is abel to force the REED VIBRATION to the natural frequency of the air column. (the reed does NOT select the freq.!)
    Compared to BRASS: the lips of the palyer have MASS which allows the player o have some control on the freq.!

    WOODWIND instruments: Tone Holes

    What happens when there are more than 1 OPEN hole?
    • If regularly spaced, they are called "tone-hole lattice"
    • Transmits waves of HIGH FREQ. and reflects back LOW FREQ.
    • Cutoff freq. : critical FREQ. above which sound waves propagate through a lattice tone holes
    - More than 1 open hole: - if reguarly spaces they are called "tone-hole lattice" and transmits waves of HIGH FREQ. but REFLECTS LOW FEQ. Cutoff freq. (deending on geometrical parameters) -> important factor of determining the TIMBER of a woodwind instrument. - the effective length depends on FREQ. as trumpet bell terminations

    WOODWIND instruments: Tone Holes

    - upper resonances are lowered slightly with respect to eh lowest TromboneSlide_positions
    - freq-ABOVE cutoff Freq. very WEAK
    - freq. above cutoff the open tone holes radiae so well that very little sound is reflected back toward the mouthpiece
    The impedance peaks are the same as a the spectrum of a Mic located INSIDE the mouthpiece. One located OUTSIDE will should better pieaks at high freq. due to the very efficient radiation from the tone holes.

    WOODWIND instruments: Tone Holes

    - the cutoff freq. of the lattice is an important FACTOR in determining the TIMBER of a woodwind inst.
    - WE can predict it and it is only a function of GEOMETRICAL parameters (size, shape and spacing)--> great for the designers!

    WOODWIND instruments: RADIATION

    - LOW freq.: UPWARDS directions potentiated
    - HIGHER: DOWNWARDS and a COMB FILTER is generated due to the very close reflections getting to the listener (so, more prominent peaks and valleys - some freq. disapear!)

    • Resonances:
      • General: non-harmonically distributed
      • Circular: series of harmonic overtones + other overtones
      • Striking away from the center: more pitch-like!

    PERCUSSION instruments: Plates

    • Plates (2D) <-> beam (1D)
      [significant bending stiffness]
    • Resonances:
      • General: non-harmonically distributed
      • Shaping (for example as church bell): series of harmonic overtones + other overtones
